Ecological Sustainability & Data Centers — Tapestry Analysis v3.3.1

About This Study

This dashboard presents results from a systematic audit of 3,044 AI governance statements worldwide, examining how they treat data-center environmental impact. Statements were scored by a reliability-tested rubric (ICC = 0.977, κ = 0.90). The headline finding: sustainability discourse is near-absent across all regions — a universal governance–reality gap, not an Anglophone privilege gap.

Background: Why data-center sustainability in AI governance?

Global AI governance addresses fairness, transparency, and accountability in detail, yet whether it confronts the material environmental burdens of AI infrastructure remains largely unexamined. Data centers consumed an estimated 460 TWh of electricity in 2024 (1.5–2% of global supply), with projections reaching 945 TWh by 2030. US data centers alone consumed 660 billion liters of cooling water in 2023. Five major hyperscalers — Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta, and Oracle — signed nuclear power agreements in 2024. This study examines a specific question: does the global corpus of AI governance statements engage substantively with these infrastructure costs, or does governance discourse systematically fail to represent the knowledge of affected communities?

The theoretical framework draws on epistemic laziness (Medina, 2013) — a dispositional failure of institutions to develop hermeneutical sensitivity toward marginalized knowledge. Unlike contributory injustice (Dotson, 2011), which requires individual willfulness, epistemic laziness scales to institutional analysis: governance-producing institutions fail to develop the interpretive resources needed to understand the environmental harms of the technology they seek to govern.

Methods: How was this study conducted?

The study uses a hybrid keyword–LLM scoring pipeline applied to the Tapestry database (Lin, 2026), a collection of 3,044 AI governance statements from ~3,460 organizations across 73 countries (1998–2026). The pipeline has three stages:

  1. Keyword pre-filter: 2,820 text extracts scanned against a 6-thread keyword architecture. 431 candidates (14.1%) passed threshold ≥ 10.
  2. LLM deep scoring: Each candidate scored by Claude across 11 sustainability dimensions (6 core threads + 5 extended dimensions) plus greenwash risk and actionability. Yields 302 nonzero, 29 Tier A (≥ 50).
  3. Contrast analysis: 21 external empirical sources — selected by a 16-member expert council — benchmark governance discourse against the current state of knowledge on disability, gendered labor, informal economy, religious ethics, and geographic burden.

Test–retest reliability: ICC(2,1) = 0.977 overall; κ = 0.90 for framing classification. False-negative rate: 2.7% (Wilson 95% CI: 0.7–9.2%). Limitation: The expert council consists of LLM-generated disciplinary perspectives, not human domain experts — creating a recursive limitation where LLM scores governance using a rubric designed through LLM-mediated deliberation.

How to read Tier A / B / C / D

Each statement is scored 0–100 on each dimension based on the depth and specificity of its sustainability engagement. Tiers classify the overall sustainability composite:

  • Tier A (≥ 50): Deep substantive engagement — addresses specific data center impacts, proposes governance mechanisms. 29 statements (0.95% of corpus).
  • Tier B (25–49): Moderate engagement with some specificity. 15 statements.
  • Tier C (10–24): Minimal — mentions sustainability as context, no substantive analysis. 100 statements.
  • Tier D (1–9): Incidental keyword match with no real content. 158 statements.
  • Zero: No sustainability content detected after LLM review. 129 statements (keyword false positives).
What This Dashboard Measures. This dashboard presents results from a systematic sustainability analysis of 3,044 AI governance statements drawn from the Tapestry database (Lin, 2026) — the most comprehensive global catalog of official AI principles and governance positions, spanning governments, corporations, civil society organizations, religious bodies, intergovernmental organizations, and professional associations from 1998 through 2026. The central finding is one of discursive absence: only 0.95% of these statements (29 out of 3,044) engage substantively with ecological sustainability — the material infrastructure that AI systems require, including energy consumption, water use, land conversion, and the labor conditions of data supply chains. This is not a study of what governance says about sustainability. It is a study of what governance systematically fails to say about the physical and environmental costs of the technology it seeks to govern.

Key Findings at a Glance
  • 0.95% substantive engagement: Only 29 of 3,044 AI governance statements achieve Tier A (deep, sustained engagement with ecological sustainability) — fewer than 1 in 100.
  • The shallow middle: 66.6% of the 431 keyword-filtered statements scored at Tier D or zero, meaning two-thirds of governance documents that mention sustainability-related terms do so without any substantive engagement.
  • The water blind spot: Only 38 statements mention water at all, and just 9 achieve high engagement — yet US data centers consumed 660 billion liters of cooling water in 2023.
  • The nuclear gap: No governance statement scores above 40 on nuclear/energy sources, despite all five major hyperscalers (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta, Oracle) signing nuclear power deals in 2024.
  • Civil society carries the load: Civil society organizations represent approximately 18% of the corpus but account for the majority of Tier A entries, an engagement ratio of roughly 4.6:1 relative to their corpus share.
  • The governance-reality paradox: Regions with the fastest data center expansion do not necessarily have stronger sustainability governance. Western Europe (21.1%) and North America (18.9%) engage at only a modestly higher rate than Central & Eastern Europe (14.7%), Southeast Asia (15.3%), and MENA (14.1%) — the gap is narrower than previously assumed, but engagement is thin across every region.
  • Disability as structural exclusion: 26 of 27 statements with any disability score receive exactly 5 (the minimum nonzero value). The corpus maximum of 20 is the sharpest epistemic boundary in the dataset.
  • One statement stands alone: STMT-2599 (Canadian Union of Public Employees) is the sole statement scoring nonzero on all three underrepresented dimensions: gendered labor (35), disability (20, the corpus maximum), and informal economy (15).
  • Greenwash follows framing: Solution-framed statements show 2.3× the greenwash risk of problem-framed statements. Dual-framed statements show the highest risk of all (mean 22.5).
  • Government silence: 90.6% of government statements adopt neutral framing despite governments being the primary regulatory authority for data center environmental impacts.

What Are “Threads”? The scoring rubric evaluates sustainability engagement across six core dimensions — each representing a distinct category of environmental impact from AI data center infrastructure. Energy captures engagement with electricity consumption, power sourcing, and grid impacts. Water measures attention to cooling water consumption and watershed effects. Nuclear/Energy Sources tracks governance of nuclear power purchase agreements and alternative baseload energy. Climate covers carbon emissions, Scope 1/2/3 footprints, and offset mechanisms. Cost Tracking assesses per-query environmental cost accounting and lifecycle analysis. Policy evaluates engagement with environmental regulation, siting requirements, and sustainability standards. Each thread is scored 0–100 based on depth and specificity of engagement.

The Nuclear Void. No governance statement scores above 40 on nuclear energy — meaning zero Tier A engagement — despite nuclear power being the primary baseload strategy pursued by all five major hyperscalers. Microsoft signed a 20-year deal to restart Three Mile Island Unit 1 (September 2024). Google partnered with Kairos Power for small modular reactors (October 2024). Amazon acquired the Cumulus Data Center campus adjacent to Talen Energy’s nuclear station. Meta issued an RFP seeking 1–4 GW of nuclear capacity (December 2024). Oracle announced a campus exceeding 1 GW powered by SMRs. This governance absence reflects three factors: temporal lag (these deals postdate most governance statements in the corpus), corpus composition (civil society organizations dominating top-scoring statements lack energy systems engineering expertise and orient advocacy toward renewables), and a measurement challenge (95.9% of initial keyword hits for “nuclear” referred to weapons proliferation, requiring extensive false-positive filtering).
The Water Blind Spot. Only 38 statements mention water at all, and just 9 achieve high engagement — yet water is one of the primary environmental impacts of data center operations. US data centers consumed 660 billion liters of cooling water in 2023. Liquid cooling systems for high-density AI workloads dramatically increase per-rack water demand. Gulf states are pursuing trillion-dollar AI data center ambitions in one of the world’s most water-stressed regions. Governance lacks hydrological expertise to articulate water consumption as a governance concern — the AI ethics community that produces most governance discourse has no tradition of engaging with water resource management.

Sustainability Mentions Over Time
The ChatGPT Inflection — Public Concern Drives Governance. The sharp acceleration from 2023 onward is not coincidental. The rapid scaling of generative AI infrastructure — massive new data center construction, rising electricity demand straining regional grids, and water consumption controversies in drought-affected communities — generated sustained public concern that catalyzed governance attention. 35 problem-framed statements emerged after 2023, compared to only 4 in all prior years combined. The 2024–2025 spike reflects a convergence of civil society advocacy campaigns, EU regulatory attention, and investigative journalism exposing the gap between corporate sustainability pledges and actual resource consumption.
The Temporal Lag Problem — Governance After the Fact. Corporate infrastructure decisions consistently outpace governance responses by 12–18 months. The clearest example: nuclear-data-center agreements (Microsoft–TMI, Google–Kairos, Amazon–Talen, Meta RFP) were all signed in Q3–Q4 2024, yet zero governance instruments have achieved Tier A engagement with nuclear energy for AI. By the time governance frameworks address nuclear-AI questions, the reactor restart decisions, site selections, and grid interconnection agreements will already be locked in. Communities affected by data center siting currently have no governance framework to draw upon when corporate announcements are made — a structural democratic deficit in anticipatory governance.

This tab examines two complementary dimensions of governance discourse: framing orientation (whether organizations present AI infrastructure as an environmental problem, solution, both, or neither) and greenwash risk (whether sustainability claims are substantive or performative). Together, these lenses reveal institutional incentives: who can afford to name environmental problems, who benefits from framing AI as a sustainability solution, and whose silence serves strategic interests. Framing Orientation by Organization Type
Four Framing Categories. Problem-framed (40 statements, 13.2%): identify AI infrastructure as an environmental burden requiring governance intervention. Solution-framed (26 statements, 8.6%): present AI as a tool for achieving sustainability goals. Both (6 statements, 2.0%): acknowledge environmental costs while simultaneously claiming AI as a sustainability solution. Neutral (230 statements, 76.2%): mention sustainability without taking an analytical position. The distribution matters because framing shapes what governance actors consider actionable.
Framing by Organization Type — Institutional Incentives at Work. Civil society (33.3% problem-framed) reflects its advocacy mission — environmental NGOs exist to identify environmental problems. Intergovernmental bodies (27.8% solution-framed) reflect institutional commitment to technology-for-development narratives. Government’s 90.6% neutrality may reflect “discursive closure” (Fricker, 2007; Dotson, 2011): government actors positioned between industry investment interests and environmental accountability narrow their governance discourse to exclude problem-identification as a risk-avoidance strategy. Only 3 government statements are problem-framed — despite governments being the primary regulatory authority for data center environmental impacts — suggesting structural suppression of governance’s problem-identification function.
Greenwash Risk by Organization Type
What Greenwash Risk Measures. The greenwash risk score (0–100) identifies statements that make sustainability claims without concrete commitments, measurable targets, or accountability mechanisms. A high score signals governance that appears substantive but lacks the specificity needed to drive meaningful environmental outcomes. High greenwash risk paired with low actionability indicates performative engagement — sustainability as brand management rather than governance.
Greenwash Follows Framing — The Central Finding. Solution-framed statements carry 2.3× higher greenwash risk (mean 14.0) than problem-framed statements (mean 6.2). Statements with dual framing — acknowledging costs while claiming AI as a solution — carry the highest risk (mean 22.5). This pattern suggests that framing AI as an environmental solution serves as a discursive mechanism for minimizing perceived environmental costs. Statements that acknowledge problems while claiming solutions are the most likely to be performative.
Tier-Framing Interaction — Depth Requires Problem Recognition. Tier A statements (deep substantive engagement) are 69% problem-framed. Tier D statements (incidental mentions) are 89.2% neutral. Substantive engagement with sustainability requires identifying environmental costs — governance that avoids problem-framing cannot achieve analytical depth. This has direct institutional implications: governance frameworks designed to be “balanced” or “stakeholder-neutral” may be structurally incapable of producing substantive sustainability analysis.

Disability: The Structural Exclusion Finding. The disability dimension reveals governance’s sharpest epistemic boundary. Of 27 statements with any disability score, 26 score exactly 5 (the minimum nonzero value). The corpus maximum is 20 — a ceiling unlike any other dimension. This is not a measurement artifact — it accurately reflects the limit of what governance institutions are capable of knowing. Two parallel causal pathways connect data centers to disability-specific harms, but no governance statement traces either:

Respiratory pathway: Data center backup diesel generators emit particulate matter and nitrogen oxides, disproportionately harming people with respiratory conditions. Ren et al. (2024) project 600,000 asthma cases and 1,300 premature deaths annually from US data center pollution by 2030. The Kapor Foundation (2025) found one-third of California data centers are in the worst 10% for diesel particulate exposure. Disadvantaged communities bear 200× the per-household environmental burden, and disabled people are overrepresented in these communities (EPA, 2016).

Grid reliability pathway: Data centers strain electrical grids, increasing outage frequency. Power outages are life-threatening for people dependent on powered medical equipment. Data center load is projected to reach 12% of US electricity by 2028.

Why governance is blind: no governance-producing institution has disability constituencies or staff expertise at the intersection of disability studies, environmental health, electrical grid engineering, and urban planning. The Lancet Planetary Health systematic review (Stein et al., 2024) found that only 1% of climate adaptation research considers disability. AI governance’s gap reflects a broader failure to connect infrastructure impacts to disability-specific harms.
Gendered Labor: Invisible Work in the AI Supply Chain. Only 25 statements engage with gendered labor (max=40), and none treat it as a central sustainability concern. Yet the evidence is clear: women face 4.7% vs 2.4% GenAI task exposure in data annotation and content moderation (Williams et al.). Platform labor is feminized and precarious (ILO/NASK). Data annotation workers experience “embedded ethics” — their labor shapes AI systems but remains invisible (Posada, 2022). Fairwork (2025) documents 31% wage theft rates in cloudwork platforms. Governance cannot see this labor because AI is conceptualized as a technology product rather than a labor process — the analytical frame excludes the human work that produces training data.
Informal Economy: The Global Burden. Only 17 statements mention the informal economy (max=25). None addresses the documented health costs of e-waste processing in the Global South, where data center hardware is dismantled by informal workers. None addresses the “ghost work” (Gray & Suri, 2019) that powers AI systems — an invisible labor force of precarious data workers, predominantly in low-income countries. The environmental and labor costs of AI infrastructure are disproportionately borne by workers who are invisible to governance — a hidden subsidy from the Global South to the Global North.
Religious Environmental Ethics: Normative Resources Ignored. Catholic integral ecology (Laudato Si’, Gozum & Eballo, 2025) frames environmental exploitation and social injustice as inseparable. Islamic trusteeship (khalifah, Ali et al., 2025) establishes humans as stewards of creation with obligations to future generations. Both traditions offer concepts — intergenerational obligation, sacred trust, interconnectedness of social and environmental harm — directly relevant to technology governance. With 53 nonzero entries (max=55), there is some engagement, but governance’s secular-technocratic framing systematically excludes these normative resources even when they offer analytical depth that secular frameworks lack.

Implications for Institutional Reform. The findings across all five underrepresented dimensions converge on a single insight: this is not an information deficit — the empirical evidence exists. It is a structural deficiency in who produces governance and what knowledge they have access to. Adding “sustainability” as a principle to existing frameworks does not address contributory injustice because governance-producing institutions still lack the constituency structures needed to identify which sustainability harms matter and to whom. Reform requires epistemic intervention — changing what governance is capable of knowing: mandating disability representation on AI ethics boards and standards-setting bodies; requiring environmental impact assessments to include disability-specific health outcomes; funding disability-led, worker-led, and community-led governance organizations. The contrast methodology developed in this study — scoring governance against external literature to identify patterned absences — is replicable across any domain where governance may systematically exclude affected communities’ knowledge: content moderation, platform labor, algorithmic decision-making, and criminal justice risk assessment.

Methodology
How This Analysis Was Conducted. This dashboard presents results from a systematic analysis of 3,044 AI governance statements from the Tapestry database (Lin, 2026). A hybrid keyword-LLM scoring pipeline first filtered statements with sustainability-relevant keywords (431 candidates, 14.1% of corpus), then scored each across 11 dimensions using a multi-prompt LLM pipeline. Of these, 302 received nonzero scores and only 29 achieved Tier A (≥50, deep engagement). The 11 dimensions span 6 core threads (energy, water, nuclear, climate, cost tracking, policy) and 5 underrepresented dimensions identified through a 16-member expert council process (environmental justice, religious/ethical ecology, gendered labor, disability, informal economy). Three additional quality indicators (greenwash risk, actionability, overall composite) assess governance quality beyond content.

Each dimension is scored 0–100 based on the depth and specificity of a statement’s engagement with that sustainability concern. 0 = no relevant content. 1–24 = passing or incidental mention (Tier C/D). 25–49 = moderate engagement (Tier B). 50–100 = substantial to core treatment (Tier A). Across scored statements, the mean is 11.8 and the median is 8. The maximum is 95.

What the tiers mean. Tier A (≥50): deep substantive engagement — addresses specific data center impacts, proposes governance mechanisms. Tier B (25–49): moderate engagement with some specificity. What “greenwash risk” means. Score identifying statements making sustainability claims without concrete commitments, measurable targets, or accountability mechanisms. Solution-framed statements show 2.3× the greenwash risk of problem-framed statements.

The 11 scored dimensions. Six core threads: energy, water, nuclear/energy sources, climate, cost tracking, policy. Five extended dimensions: environmental justice, religious/ethical ecology, gendered labor, disability, informal economy.

Hybrid Keyword + LLM Scoring Pipeline

Phase 1 — Keyword Pre-Filter: 2,820 text extracts scanned against 6-thread keyword architecture. Scoring: strong (×15) + medium (×5) − false-positive context (×10) + title bonus (20), clamped [0,100]. Co-occurrence boosting upgrades medium→strong when contextually validated.

Phase 2 — LLM Deep Read: 351 candidates (keyword score ≥ 10) sent to Claude for contextual analysis. LLM scores 6 threads + 5 additional dimensions (justice, ethics/religious, gendered labor, disability, informal economy) plus greenwash risk (0-100) and actionability (0-100). LLM scores override keywords where they differ.

Expert Council

16-member council: 12 domain experts (climate, water, energy, nuclear, policy, economics, justice, measurement, corporate sustainability, AI systems, infrastructure, toxicology) + 4 ethics/religious voices (environmental ethics, Abrahamic traditions, Eastern/Indigenous traditions, theology & technology).

Source Corpus

Tapestry — The Global AI Principles & Governance Database (Lin, 2026). ~3,044 statements from ~3,460 organizations across all sectors and regions. Text extracts available for ~2,820 statements.

Dimension Count Note

Main tabs filter to sust_overall > 0. The Missing Voices tab includes all statements with nonzero dimension scores regardless of overall score.

Scoring Reliability and What Low Reliability Reveals. The scoring pipeline achieves excellent reliability on core dimensions: ICC(2,1) = 0.977 overall, 0.987 for energy, 0.983 for climate, 0.949 for water. However, disability (ICC = 0.13) and informal economy (ICC = 0.065) show near-floor reliability — not because measurement is unstable, but because the distributions are near-zero (almost no governance content exists to score). Low ICC on these dimensions is itself a meaningful finding: it reflects genuine epistemic absence in governance, not instrument failure.
Pipeline Efficiency and the False Negative Tradeoff. The hybrid approach reduces computational cost by 85% compared to scoring all 3,044 statements with LLM. The keyword filter’s false-negative rate is 2.7% (Wilson 95% CI: 0.7–9.2%), meaning approximately 8 statements may contain sustainability content missed by keyword pre-filtering. The one confirmed rescue (STMT-2663, UNEP, score=90) is exceptional; FN-recovered statements tend to score lower.
Critical Limitations. Three limitations readers should bear in mind: (1) The 16-member “council” consists of LLM-generated personas, not human domain experts — creating a recursive limitation where LLM scores governance using a rubric designed through LLM-mediated deliberation. (2) Only 1 of 21 contrast sources was authored by affected communities; 20 were authored about affected populations — the study partially reproduces the epistemic asymmetry it identifies. (3) Eight environmental impact categories fall outside the rubric’s scope: land use change, e-waste, critical mineral extraction, toxic air emissions (NOx, PM2.5), fluorinated gas leakage, biodiversity impacts, thermal pollution, and noise pollution.
Contrast Methodology — Measuring What Governance Omits. The study’s analytical framework is grounded in epistemic injustice theory (Dotson, 2011; Fricker, 2007). 21 external sources across 5 thematic streams (disability & environmental burden, gendered labor in data supply chains, informal economy impacts, religious environmental ethics, geographic/environmental burden) provide benchmarks against which governance is evaluated. The approach asks: whose knowledge is structurally excluded and what institutional mechanisms produce that exclusion? When 0 of 3,044 statements address disability and environmental burden together, this is not an oversight — it reflects whose voices are present in governance and whose are not.

Worst-Case False Negative Impact

At the 95% CI upper bound FN rate (9.2%, vs. 2.7% observed), up to 220 additional statements from the zero-scoring pool (N=2,389) could enter the scored pool. Proportional estimates suggest up to 15 additional Tier A and 8 additional Tier B statements. However, FN-recovered statements tend to score lower — the one confirmed FN rescue (STMT-2663, UNEP, overall=90) is exceptional. Realistically, the Tier A count could range from 29 to ~35–40, but the distribution of themes and org types would remain stable. Key findings (civil society dominance, energy-policy co-occurrence) are robust.
